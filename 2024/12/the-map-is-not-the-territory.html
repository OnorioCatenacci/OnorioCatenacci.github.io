The Map Is Not The Territory

2024-12-20 :: SoftwareEngineering, PhilosophicalMusings

By: Onorio Catenacci

The map is not the territory. —Alfred Korzybski

Our models are wrong. They can never be right. What can we do to deal with this?

It’s sometimes wise to remind ourselves of facts that seem so obvious as to need no remark. “Estimates are not commitments” is something I’m sure every software developer in the world wishes were plastered on every wall of every managers’ office.

As you can see above there’s also the famous observation by Polish philosopher Korzybski—“the map is not the territory”. Or stated another way closer to the way software developers think—your requirements are not reality. Another terrific demonstration of this idea is The Treachery of Images by the Belgian surrealist painter Ren√© Magritte. In case you’re unable to follow the link, it’s a painting of what’s clearly a tobacco pipe with the legend under the image “This is not a pipe”. Magritte’s statment is completely correct—not a pipe. A painting of a pipe.

I’d even go a step further and say “the map can never be the territory”. It doesn’t matter how detailed the map is, it’s not the ground. It’s not a question of accuracy; it’s a question of perception. Birds can see UV light—we cannot. We can shine UV light on to objects but we’re still not seeing UV light; what we’re seeing is the light waves in the spectrum visible to us which are not absorbed by the object. We’re certainly not seeing the UV reflected back because we can’t see it.

Someone created a sculpture of a tesseract which is a four dimensional object. Is it the tesseract? Nope. It’s a three-dimensional representation of a tesseract. While that may sound like a hair-splitting distinction it the same distinction as “the map is not the territory”. And becase we don’t possess the organs of perception we can’t even perceive the territory—well not directly anway.

So when we’re creating models of systems for end-users—well no matter how good their perception the model isn’t reality. No matter how we try the model cannot be the reality.

So what does this imply for software development? I’d say it implies a few things:

1.) Your model isn’t perfect. It never will be. Stop trying to make it perfect.

2.) The software built from your imperfect model will also be imperfect. Accept this and do the best you can.

3.) Since we cannot perceive all the details of reality we need to model, we need to prioritize the most important details to ensure they’re addressed. Of course this is the tricky part because what seems important to the software developer is often not what’s important to the person paying the bills.

4.) While some things are possible they’re not probable. If I were building a skyscraper I need to account for potential lightning strikes. Not an if, a when.

I might also want to account for the possibility of a meteor striking my skyscraper. Guess which of those two is the higher priority to plan for and deal with?
